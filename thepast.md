## Eras

### Born — 1967-01-27, 1:35 p.m.

I was born in Poughkeepsie, New York, on January 27, 1967, at 1:35 p.m. at St. Francis Hospital — room 111, nurse 1. My name is **John Jeffery Kyzer**. My father is **John Stephen Kyzer**; my mother is **Janice Carol Remington**.

---

### Early childhood & before high school — ?–~1978

There are a few things I remember from growing up before high school; I will come back to them later.

---

### Childhood & school years (pre–high school) — ~1973–~1979

I was not one of the popular kids. I pretty much did what I needed to do to get by. I was very shy — I have said several times that I was *debilitatingly* shy — partly because I was always getting into trouble for something.

I do remember good times growing up, but I was often in trouble: yelled at, grounded, spanked when I was younger, degraded in some manner. I feel it was this way of growing up that made me shy as I got older. As I aged, I did what I could to keep people from being upset with me; but like my grades at school, I did what I needed to do to get by.

I had two friends in school I clearly remember: **Frank Mersand** and **Matt Davis**. I am sure I had others, but I do not really remember who they were.

We lived on **nine acres** of land in rural New York, just outside Poughkeepsie. My father’s property was right next to a large farm, and I spent plenty of time on the farm property.

Although my father tried to get me interested in cars, hunting, and fixing things, I was never very interested. I did learn a few things from him — for example, you will never really know what is wrong with something until you open it up and look inside. I find that an interesting thought now that I am writing this. I also think I learned that there is a basic logical process to any objective — though that learning may have only been the start of that lesson.

---

### High school — ~1979–1985

I was on the **track team** — long-distance running and the long jump. I remember classes like physics, writing, and learning the typewriter. I was scolded often for “not living up to my potential,” even though one teacher labeled me a **space cadet**. Looking back, she was correct.

I took the school bus every day — almost the first one on and the last one off.

In the last couple of years of high school I worked at a grocery store, **The Great Atlantic and Pacific Tea Company** (the **A&P**). My first car was a **canary-yellow Chevy Monza**.

#### Jill Montano

At the A&P I met my first girlfriend, **Jill Montano**. She was hired as a checker — she rang people up and charged them for what they bought. I was a stock boy. One day they called for stock boys to help bag because the store was busy. I went to help Jill. She smiled at me and said hi, and I fell in love — or maybe infatuation, or puppy love. I was eighteen-ish, I think. After that day I made sure I was her bagger.

Most weekends we would have pizza, go to a movie, and make out for a while somewhere before I had to get her home. We did other typical high-school-couple things as well.

**Matt Davis** and I played **racquetball**. We would go to the **Beekman rec** center, where there were a couple of **outdoor racquetball courts** we played on.

---

### Navy — enlistment, departure, boot camp — 1984–1985

#### Choosing the Navy

In the **summer of 1984** I joined the Navy. I remember getting phone calls from the Marines and the Army, but when the Navy called, I knew what I was doing after high school. When I was younger I knew two things: I did not want to have children, and I was going to be on the big ships. I finished high school knowing what came next.

#### A&P night shift (mistake)

Still working at the A&P before I left for boot camp, they needed people on the **night shift**. I did that for about the week before I left for the Navy. That was a mistake. I found myself falling asleep when I was trying to spend time with Jill. I am not sure why I did not just quit the job two or three weeks before boot camp. I guess it was a feeling of responsibility at an early age — they needed me at the store and I needed the money. Thinking back, it really did not matter: the store would have survived without me, and I could have asked my parents for money. I am guessing they would have given me a hard time about it but would have given it to me nonetheless. In the end it did not really matter. I wish that lesson had stuck earlier: **what matters and what does not**.

#### Leaving home — 1985-07-25

On **July 25, 1985**, I was on my way to **Orlando, Florida** — my first time away from anything I recognized as home.

#### Boot camp — Orlando, summer 1985

Boot camp was boot camp. It was Florida in August and it was hot. The food was the food, and we were always doing something — trying not to fall asleep or get in trouble so we would not have to do more push-ups.

I did like **marching in file**. I found it fun and interesting to be part of a group of individuals that acted as one unit when everything went right.

In boot camp I was on the lower end of the pecking order, much like high school — but I do not remember caring much. I was just trying to get through it all.

We seemed to be in trouble every day — fake trouble, or motivational trouble — as opposed to “real” trouble. I did get in real trouble once.

**Jill** came to see me graduate. We spent the day together and into the evening. I was supposed to be back at the barracks before 11 p.m., I think — I had even planned to be back by 10:30. Either way, I was late because the taxi I had reserved did not show up. I was going to be okay until our commander noticed that my **T-shirt was on backwards**. I was sent back a week and did not graduate with my class. That ended up being a good thing, but that was for later.

I had to go back to my original barracks to get my peacoat. There was a big board on the wall that showed each person and their stats — PT test, class grades, other things like that. When someone dropped out or was removed from the platoon, their name was crossed off in red all the way across the board. Such was my name. I remember crying the whole way back to my new platoon.

Boot camp ended and I headed to **Pensacola, Florida** — **Corry Station** — for my A-school.

#### Corry Station — holding, watch, class (~1985–1986)

I never really saw much of Pensacola. I think I left the base twice.

For the **first few months** I was in a holding pattern, waiting to be assigned a school date. Every day we did some kind of chore on the base — mow lawns, paint, mostly general cleanup — and then we stood watch. Watch was only **two hours** instead of four, to help us get ready for the real thing.

##### Parking lot watch — Super Bowl night

I remember standing **parking lot watch**: you basically walked around the parking lot for two hours, checking in every fifteen minutes or so. That night the **Chicago Bears won the Super Bowl** — Super Bowl XX, **January 1986** *(you recalled 1996; the Bears’ win that fits this period is 1986)*. It was also the coldest Florida had been in a long time — mid to upper 30s. The cold had brought an unusual fog into the area. The fog sat at about **thirty feet** above the ground: clear below, foggy above. The lights were just inside the fog layer, which gave the parking lot an eerie, surreal look. I remember thinking I really wished I had a camera. My enjoyment of **photography** was stimulated that night.

##### Office days & Sharon

When you first arrive at the base, everyone spends a few days in the **office** helping out — so you can get to know the officers, get a feel for the military outside of boot camp, and the general flow of how things work there.

**Sharon** was awesome. We were in about the same place in the military: we had thought it was where we belonged, but at some point realized that might not be the case. We wanted to be back home with the people and boyfriends or girlfriends — but here we were. We took a liking to each other right away. She was from **Portland, Oregon**.

It was a Friday afternoon when another person stuck their head in the office and said, “Nawlins, 16:30,” and left. Sharon looked at me and asked, “Have you ever been to Nawlins?” I had been out of New York only two or three times before, and New Orleans was not one of them. I said I did not even know where that was. She explained it was **New Orleans** and told me to go pack for two nights in a different city.

At **16:30** about a dozen of us were packed into **three cars**, heading west on **I-10**. When we got there, two people acting as a couple went into the **Clarion Hotel** just outside the Quarter and got a room; the rest of us crashed it. It was fun and pretty exciting — I had never done anything like that before. I was also trying to **keep a low profile** and not upset anyone, as I had learned earlier. Something I wish I had not done; it would have been more fun.

We walked around the **French Quarter**, had **beignets at Café du Monde**, and I saw the **Mississippi River** up close for the first time, as well as ocean liners. I got drunk for the **second time** and drank too much — the first time I had **Cajun** cooking. It was exciting to explore the unknown. I did get too drunk; it is only **one of two times** I ever do not remember what happened because I drank too much. Somewhere there is a picture of me hanging on a **Bourbon Street** light-post sign.

On **Sunday morning**, as we were about to head back to Pensacola, a bright light woke me up and I started complaining about it. Someone said that was not a light — it was the **sun**, and it was time to go. I got my arms under me to lift myself up and crashed my head into the **dresser** — I had somehow gotten myself under it, probably because there were a dozen people in a **one-bedroom** hotel room and it may have been the only space I could find. We packed up and darted out of the room just as the maids were coming down the hall. We tried to be slick and stealthy so as not to get caught with that many people in one room — again, something we thought was important at the time but really was not.

##### Class date, evening school, barracks

At some point I got tired of the daily chores and asked when my class would start. I think they had just **lost track of me** — I recall someone asking why I had been there for **four months or so** without a class date. I got a class date and ended up in the **evening class**, which turned out to be a bit valuable.

Remember I said being dropped from my boot camp platoon ended up with some benefits? At Corry Station the barracks were **three or four stories**. The women were on the first floor and the men on the others — except for **two rooms** on the first floor that, because of where the doors were for the women’s section, were accessible to men. I ended up in one of those rooms. It was a **quad**, but I do not remember there ever being more than two of us in that room for most of my stay.

##### First bank account, loan, and pay

Corry Station was the **first time I had a bank account** and the **first time I took out a loan**. I borrowed so I could buy a **plane ticket to New York** for the **holidays**. The loan was paid back from my paycheck **before I got my paycheck** — taken out of pay automatically.

I also started putting my money into the bank. Before that I was paid **in cash** on the **1st and the 15th**, I think — **$102** a pay. That was **less** than I made at the A&P, which I believe was **$2.65 an hour** in 1984.

##### Racquetball, weight lifting, and flying “back in time”

I **continued playing racquetball** at Corry Station — the same sport **Matt Davis** and I had played in high school at Beekman rec.

I also **started weight lifting** there. I thought I would get buff to look better for **Jill**. The weight lifting came and went in **spurts**.

Whenever I went **home** for a visit, I would plan to come back on a **Monday**. Since I did not have to be at class until later in the afternoon (evening class), I could catch a flight out of **New York** for **Atlanta**. The fun part: **Atlanta is in the Eastern time zone** and **Pensacola is in the Central time zone**. The flight from Atlanta to Pensacola was **less than an hour**. I would leave around **11 a.m.** and get to Pensacola around **10:50 a.m.** — more or less going **back in time**.

I was always able to get back to Corry Station, get ready for class, and be there well before I had to — which meant **more time in New York** with family and Jill. I always found that interesting and fun. **Time and space** were starting to be interesting to me and to have more meaning than just *where are you* and *what time is it*.

##### Integrity — the plane ticket

Something I am not proud of: when one of the guys in my room was leaving Corry Station for good, they had left their **plane ticket** on the table. They were gone and were not coming back, so I grabbed the ticket and put it in my locker. They did end up coming back to get it. When I gave it to them there was a look of **distrust** as to why I had put it in my locker. I said I was holding it for safekeeping — but I had already planned to bring it back wherever and get the **money for it**. Basically stealing it. Of course I did not think about the fact that they could have just been reissued a ticket. Either way, that feeling stuck with me. Although I may have found stuff that did not belong to me in my possession since, that feeling of letting someone down stuck with me, and I pretty much stuck to **not stealing**. This would later evolve into a personal characteristic of **integrity** that I try to uphold.

##### Weed whacker & the car window

At one point, while doing daily chores, I was **weed-whacking** grass in the parking lot. According to someone, a rock had come off the weed whacker and hit a car window, which broke. They told me I needed to tell the person in charge of the detail so it could get fixed. Afraid of getting into trouble, I did not — even after being told I would **not** get into trouble. Eventually the person who told me reported it themselves. I never really got into trouble, but it **felt** like trouble nonetheless.

##### Graduation & duty station — Mayport

Corry Station ended with **graduation** from the class. The way someone got their duty-station assignment was by how well they did in class. There were **eleven** of us and **eleven billets**. I was in the **middle** of the class — par for the course.

I knew I wanted to go to the **Mediterranean**, and there were two ships and three billets on the East Coast that were going to have a Med tour — one in **Norfolk** and one in **Mayport, Florida**. I got the one in **Mayport**, along with **John Tillman** — a big lummox of a guy I will go into more detail about later.

From one side of northern Florida to the other, I headed to **Mayport** and the **USS *Charles F. Adams* (DDG-2)**.

### USS *Charles F. Adams* (DDG-2), Mayport — ~1986–1988-04-22

I got to my ship already **kind of in trouble**. I was supposed to show up in **dress uniform**. There was some question as to why I was not. I had been told **not to** by my **petty officer**, because the ship was having some **major work** done.

I **left the ship on April 22, 1988**. In that time:

- **Port-au-Prince, Haiti** — first place I went outside the U.S. (see below).
- **Caribbean drug-interdiction ops** (~mid-1980s; e.g. **October 1986**) — search for drug ships; see below.
- Sailed all around the **Caribbean** — although we never stopped at any other island port calls.
- **Wilmington, North Carolina**.
- **Med tour** (~**January 1987**, about **six months**): **Spain**, **Italy**, **Israel**, **Egypt** — *story not written yet; see [Stories to fill in later](#stories-to-fill-in-later)*.

#### Learning the ship — and the chance I passed up

There was a lot to learn about the ship and how to work together as a team — although in the beginning I did not see it that way. I was still trying to do what was **necessary to get by**.

Within the **first week** there was an opportunity to start moving past that. Someone asked if anyone would **stay after working hours** to help one of the guys who had **duty** that day and could not leave the ship. We were waiting for some kind of equipment and help was needed.

I almost said, “Okay, I am the new guy — I will stay and help.” But then one of the other people in my shop said something like, “Remember what Navy stands for — **Never Again Volunteer Yourself**.” I did not say anything.

I was also trying to make friends on the ship. One of them was **Mike Melton** — about my age, same rank. We were going to go out and get some food or something. If I stayed behind to help, I thought I would **miss out** on that. Looking back, I wish I had stayed and helped. Mike and I could have gotten together later that evening or the next night. That opportunity to help a fellow teammate was **lost**.

Although there were more chances to help later, I missed a moment that would have **defined myself** — not only to other people on the team, but to myself and the path I would have taken, or at least might have taken. In the end I do not know if my helping would have mattered at all, but I remember this for some reason and would have taken a **different path** if I could go back and do it again.

#### By myself — not always by choice

One thing that became part of the Navy was **doing things by myself**. A constant theme: I was by myself not because I always wanted to be, but because I would be **waiting for people to ask me** to join them, or I was **too shy to ask** people to join me. I wanted to walk out to the beach or go to the mall or whatever — so I would go and do that. This has been a **constant theme** even now.

---

#### Haiti — Port-au-Prince

**Haiti** was the **first place I went outside the United States**. I grew up on a farm and I still experienced **culture shock** — how people were living, their organization or the lack of it. There were **monkeys in the trees**, and **pigs, cats, and feral dogs** just everywhere.

We were the **first ship in a while** to dock there. All the merchant people came out in their boats with their wares to sell. The boats were basically **canoes**. They would sit off the back of the ship holding up whatever goods they wanted to sell. We were told **not to buy wood products** because they might have had a bug we did not want on the ship. *(I still have a wooden bowl and a duck.)*

##### The duck in my lap

I got the duck from a merchant in town. We were going off to a beach, so we got a **driver** to take us there. We were waiting for someone to open the gates to the dock so we could drive out. There was a **fence** around the dock area to keep people from getting down the dock to board the ship or stand there trying to sell us something.

I was in the **front seat**. It was hot and I had the window rolled down. When the gate opened, a few people rushed in showing us their goods. One guy **threw a carved duck in my lap**. I asked how much. He said **$35**. I told him I would not give him **$5** for it — although I told him I would not buy it for $5, **I still did**, and that is what the crowd wanted to see: a **willing buyer**. Junk of all types started flowing into the car. Next thing I knew there was a **pile of junk in my lap**. Everyone in the car seemed annoyed, but I thought it was **funny**.

About then it was okay for us to leave and the driver just started driving. People were in the way but he just drove; they got out of the way. At the same time we were **throwing the goods back out of the car**, not really caring what condition they would be in or if the owner would get them back. I remember laughing at the **absurdity** of it all.

##### Rum factory & jungle

Another trip was to a **rum factory** — up in the hills, a bit of a drive, so I got to see some of the **countryside**. It was the **first time I saw the jungle** and I enjoyed it greatly. We did the tour of the rum factory, which was very interesting, then headed back to the **tasting room**, where we had the opportunity to taste all their rums — **23 different flavors**. We drank them all. Damn, I was **hammered**.

##### Painting the hull

Another thing I remember from Haiti was **painting the ship** — at least the **hull**. The Haitians had boats, and it was a lot easier to paint down to the **waterline** from boats. The ship hired a bunch of them, paying **$2 a foot**. The ship was **427 feet long** — or about **900 feet** if you included both sides and the stern. I thought that was interesting: we got what we needed done much faster, and we were able to **support people in the community** in a way other than buying trinkets.

#### Drinking *(theme that starts here)*

**Drinking would become a theme in my life.** I do not think I was ever an alcoholic, but I did drink a lot and to excess. It was not so much about getting drunk — it was more about the **drinking itself**, if that makes sense.

Growing up in New York, my **father drank often**. He was actually a **functioning alcoholic** at one point. I do not know if he drank to get drunk or because that is just what you do — but he drank after work and in the garage, at parties, while mowing the yard, and while fishing. Maybe I got some of that from him.

---

#### Caribbean drug ops — hand-to-hand, fresh fish, World Series *(~October 1986)*

We were often sent into the **Caribbean** to search for **drug ships**. It was kind of an interesting thing. We had some **Coast Guard** people on board who were very good at **hand-to-hand combat in tight quarters** — much like what you might find on a ship transporting drugs. It was the **mid-1980s** and the **war on drugs** was in full swing.

On some evenings we would be asked to be **dummies** and practice with them. *Dummies* was a good word for it. No matter how hard we tried to escape, we always ended up worse off, with a few **bruises** to show for the experience.

Two things stood out from those drug-ops trips.

**Fresh fish.** When we would “pull over” a fishing vessel, the boats were often **not** running drugs but actually fishing. The captains would be happy to see us because they knew the **U.S. Navy** was in the area. Piracy was not a big thing, but I am sure knowing the Navy was in their waters helped them sleep a bit sounder. I think that led to their **generosity** — or maybe we were just buying fresh fish — but more than a few times we would get **buckets of huge shrimp and prawns**, all kinds of fish and tuna. Whole tuna, sometimes still **flopping around on the deck**, would be tossed up and we knew we were going to eat well that night. That is one thing about the Navy: **they know how to feed you**. If you are well fed, the annoyances of life onboard did not seem so bad.

**October 1986 — Cuba TV and the World Series.** One such drug-ops trip was in **October of 1986**. We were out looking for drug runners throughout the day, but in the **evenings** we would come in **closer to Cuba** so we could pick up **local television** and watch the baseball **World Series**. Another thing I found interesting about **time and place**: there was no **satellite television** or anything like that, but we knew that if we got close enough to Cuba, we could pick up their local TV transmissions.

It was the **New York Mets** and the **Boston Red Sox**. The Red Sox were still under the **Curse of the Bambino**, still in one of the longest droughts without a playoff championship. There were about **300 people** on board the ship and about **200** of them were on the **mess decks**, where there were a few televisions. I think the mess decks were only supposed to hold **75 people** at a time, tops.

When the ball bounced between **Bill Buckner**’s legs, there were **two people** who jumped up and cheered. Just two. It was pretty funny to think about now — how **quiet** the crowd was, with just two guys cheering.
