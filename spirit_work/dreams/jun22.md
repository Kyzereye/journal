# 📅 06-22
**Maze bus, ski walls, and wrong roads in New York with Casey**

## The dream

This was a long series of connected dreams over one night. You remember three main parts.

### Part one: the bus in the snow

It started as a maze dream. You were traveling somewhere. It was dark and snowing. You were supposed to take the bus, but the bus was taking too long. You volunteered to help by driving the bus. You never remember actually driving it, but you do remember being inside it. You did your shift and planned to help again the next day, but they did not need your help. You were then outside the bus watching it move away without you. You felt a bit alone.

After that you were with a woman who had a clever way of telling you she wanted you to kiss her. You saw her beneath you, as if she had her head in your lap and was ready for a kiss. You do not remember kissing her, but you do remember enjoying the fact that she was asking and that you did not have to chase her.

### Part two: skiing, walls, and the car in snow

In the next part there were three of you: yourself, a woman, and another man. You were going skiing. You were in a small car parked at the ski resort. Getting to the lifts was cumbersome because you had to climb over a wall and then up a ladder attached to a different wall. You think you went skiing, but you never saw yourself actually skiing.

At the end of the day you had to climb back down the ladder and over the wall. You decided it was easier to get through these obstacles without your skis on. After the last wall you got distracted by something you were watching. You do not know what it was. At some point you remembered that you needed to catch up to the others.

When you caught up, they had already gotten the car out of a snowbank, or it had been covered with a lot of snow and was now clear. Something had made a lot of scrapes across the passenger side where you were standing. The car still drove, but you used caution. The other man rode with some other woman. When you got back to what you think was her place, the woman in your group who had driven wondered whether the other man had gotten home. You noticed snow in the parking space next to yours and pointed it out, saying you thought he had been there and had left again. There was no car in the adjacent spot, but you figured he had been there and gone.

You remember being annoyed at him because you thought he had had sex with the other woman and you had not. Or maybe it was only that he got the other woman's attention and you did not. You are not sure which.

### Part three: driving with Casey in New York

In the last part you were driving with your friend Casey. You were in New York. It was sunny and summertime. You were heading out for a day of doing fun things. You were driving on a road and knew you needed to get on a different road. You made a turn but realized it was taking you toward the highway, which you did not want. The road intersections were very confusing.

You remember seeing a map of the roads as if you were looking down on a satellite image of the intersection. Roads went in all directions. You started onto a highway entry ramp even though it was not the way you wanted to go. You were speeding up when you saw on the map that the ramp you were on was a dead end. You had not really seen the dead end in the road itself until then. Then you saw a chain-link fence through the windshield. It was kind of an overlook, and you could see a mountainside view similar to the Hudson Valley landscape where you grew up. You stopped, got turned around, and tried to figure out how to get back where you wanted to be. You figured you needed to pull over and get out the map to see which way to go.

At that point you noticed that your front driver's-side tire was flat. There was some trying to figure out what to do, but you knew it had to be fixed. You got yourselves to a shop to see if they could help. Your father was there talking with someone who worked at the shop. He was telling the shop worker something and used the number 18. You thought he was talking about the gas mileage you got in your car, but that was wrong because your actual mileage was much better, around 35 mpg.

The dream ended there.

## Analysis

### Where this sits in your maze and transport dreams

You named the first part correctly. It belongs to the maze family you have been having since March and especially since May 30: traveling with others toward something not fully named, bad weather, delays, effort, partial progress, and no clean finish. May 30 used dark rain and tunnels. This night uses dark snow and a bus that will not move fast enough.

The bus is collective transport, like the trains in May 20 and June 1. You are not supposed to be the driver. When the group system stalls, you step up and volunteer. That matches waking life lately: you are building your own company, driving shifts others used to drive, taking responsibility when the usual route is too slow. The dream honors that move. You get inside the bus and you complete a shift.

Then the dream takes the help away. They do not need you the next day. The bus leaves while you stand outside and watch it go. You feel alone. That is a sharp version of a pattern that runs through your transport dreams: you approach the vehicle, you even serve on it, but you often do not see yourself fully driving, boarding, or arriving. May 22 skipped getting in and out of the truck. May 20 never showed the train. Here you remember being in the bus but not remembering the act of driving, and then you are explicitly not on it when it moves.

The message may not be that your help failed. It may be that the psyche is tracking what happens after you prove yourself useful. The group continues without you. The journey does not owe you a permanent seat just because you drove one shift. That can land as loneliness, but it can also land as a question about whether you are trying to earn a place on someone else's carrier instead of building your own.

### The woman who asks for the kiss

This scene is short but it breaks a pattern you have named in other dreams and in waking life. You often experience desire, approval, or opportunity as something you have to chase, prepare for, or wait to be allowed. Here the woman signals clearly. She puts herself in a position where the invitation is obvious. You do not remember kissing her, but you remember liking that you did not have to pursue.

That is a different emotional receipt than the bus scene. On the bus you were useful and then left behind. With the woman you were wanted without chasing. The dream may be contrasting two kinds of arrival: one through service to the group, one through being chosen. You have been working hard on business, trips, fences, and launches. The kiss scene may be reminding you that part of the life you want includes ease on the receiving end, not only effort on the giving end.

### Part two: fun behind walls, skiing without seeing yourself ski

The ski section is another maze in recreation clothes. Three people, a small car, a resort that should be straightforward, and yet the path to the lifts requires climbing over a wall and up a ladder on another wall. The fun is real but the access is awkward. You think skiing happened, but again you never saw your body doing the thing. The dream shows approach, obstacle, and aftermath more than the activity itself.

Your practical choice matters. You took off your skis to get through the return obstacles more easily. That is adaptive. You are not failing the mountain. You are using the method that fits the wall and the ladder. That rhymes with June 14, where circular scrubbing actually removed the crust, and with your waking habit of finding a workable motion instead of the heroic one.

The distraction after the last wall is important. You were watching something and lost track of the group. When you caught up, they had already solved the car problem without you. The vehicle had been stuck in snow or buried in snow and was now clear. The passenger side had scrapes, but the car still ran. You rode with caution. The other man left with a different woman.

The ending is social more than logistical. You are annoyed, but you are honest that you are not sure why. You think he may have had sex with the other woman while you did not. Or he may only have gotten her attention while you did not. Either way the sting is comparison inside a trio. You helped with the day, navigated the walls, and still ended up feeling passed over when pairing shifted at the end.

Snow in the adjacent parking space becomes your clue that he was there and left. There was no car when you looked, but you inferred his presence from traces. That is a small version of how you read signs in waking life: not always a body at the door, but evidence that someone was there.

### Part three: Casey, New York, maps, dead ends, and the flat tire

Part three is the only section of the night where you are clearly the driver on an ordinary road with a friend beside you, in daylight, on a day meant for fun. That alone separates it from the bus in snow and the ski trio. The psyche is not only showing you stuck. It is showing you what kind of travel you actually want: summer, New York, Casey, no crisis tone, a plan to enjoy the day. After parts one and two, where you were useful and then left behind or annoyed at the end of a group outing, this scene asks what forward motion would feel like if it were chosen for pleasure rather than for proof.

Casey has not appeared in your dream file before. A friend in the passenger seat matters because recent dreams have put other men in competitive positions, not cooperative ones. The ski scene ended with another man getting the woman or the attention. The bus scene ended with the vehicle leaving without you. Casey is company without rivalry. The dream may be naming a kind of male friendship that does not require you to drive a shift for the group or lose at the end of the day. What that friendship means in waking life is still for you to fill in, but the dream uses Casey as a companion on a pleasure trip, not as a rival or an authority.

New York here is not the heavy homestead and father-property knot from May 22. It is closer to May 15’s Hudson geography, but with a different emotional weather. May 15 mixed Taconic curves, fading forward sight, and the question of whether to visit KC. June 22 gives you sun, summer, and a fun day already agreed on. The regional pull is present without the burden of deciding to move back. The dream seems to be saying that origin country can be part of a good day, not only part of a trap or a deferred inheritance story.

The highway is the part of the road system you already know you do not want. February 15 used the highway as the main route everyone piles onto while standstill traffic blocks the path. You took the empty lane instead, and it still did not connect you to where you meant to go. In June 22 you are more explicit: you turn, realize the turn feeds the highway, and resist that direction. The psyche is drawing a line between the fast collective route and the day you and Casey actually planned. In waking terms that can map to anything that feels like the standard lane — full-time employment, hustle logic, the crowded departure everyone else is taking — versus the slower local road of your own business, your trips, your chosen pace. You are not refusing all motion. You are refusing the wrong scale of motion.

The map and the windshield do different jobs, and the dream is precise about which one does which. From above you see that the ramp is a dead end while you are still speeding up. That is the mind knowing before the body accepts. You have lived this recently in waking life: you can run the numbers on Belize, see that the South Pacific desire statement allows more flexibility than scarcity allows, or know that another launch delay is avoidance, and still keep accelerating into the plan because the overview has not yet become felt truth. The map is not wrong. It is early. You do not really believe the dead end until the fence appears in front of the car.

The fence through the windshield is the moment the abstract warning becomes physical. What makes this dead end unusual in your file is what sits behind the fence. It is not a blank wall or a generic failure. It is an overlook with a mountainside view like the Hudson Valley where you grew up. May 15 put you on curving parkway roads through that same inner region while forward sight faded and backward light still worked. June 22 puts the block at a scenic point over familiar country. That suggests the ramp you are on does not dump you into foreign punishment. It stops you where you can see home terrain clearly. The message may not be “never go back.” It may be “this particular entrance to the fast road ends at a view of where you came from, and you cannot drive through the fence into the highway from here.” Past and origin are visible at the exact point forward motion is refused.

That reading also complicates the idea that the dead end is only a mistake. An overlook is a place people go on purpose to see. You arrived at it by the wrong ramp, but what you see from there is not worthless. The chain-link fence still stops the car. Beauty does not make the ramp a through road. The dream may be insisting that you can honor roots and landscape without treating this ramp as your forward exit. Looking at the valley is not the same as living there again, and it is not the same as boarding the highway either.

After you stop and turn around, you decide you need to pull over and read the map properly. That is the first wise move in the scene: not more acceleration, but orientation. The flat tire hits immediately after that decision, on the front driver’s side. You are the one steering in this part of the night, and the failure is on your wheel, not on the passenger side scrapes from part two or the bus that left without you. In dream logic, a flat at the moment of reorientation often means the vehicle of your life cannot keep rolling on habit even when you have finally chosen to stop and figure out direction. The tire does not puncture while you are blundering up the ramp. It punctures when you admit you need the map. Something in you may have to come to a full stop — not a scenic wrong turn, but actual maintenance — before the fun day with Casey can continue.

You still get to the shop. That matters. The scene does not end in the ditch or on the ramp. It ends where repair is possible. The day is interrupted, not cancelled.

Your father is already there when you arrive, talking to a worker, not to you first. He uses the number 18. You hear it as a comment on your gas mileage and assume he is measuring your car’s economy. He is not. Your real mileage is much better, around thirty-five. The misread is part of the scene, not a footnote. Father in your file often carries old scripts: property, homestead, how a man should perform, what counts as enough. May 22 tied him to NY land and waiting. Here he is adjacent to the repair you need, but he is speaking in a number that does not apply to your actual vehicle. The dream may be separating two things that get tangled in waking life: the voice that judges whether you are running efficiently enough, and the fact that your present machine performs better than that voice assumes. You are at the shop for a flat tire. He is talking about the wrong metric. If you spend the scene arguing mileage, you miss the repair.

Taken together, part three is not a repeat of “wrong turn, bad luck.” It is a compressed story about wanting a good day in familiar country with a friend, refusing the highway scale, learning from map and body in that order, being stopped at a homeland overlook you cannot drive through, choosing to orient, and meeting maintenance plus paternal mismeasurement at the place where help actually exists. The question it leaves open is not whether you can drive. You already were. The question is whether you will fix the tire and let the day resume, or stay in the argument about eighteen versus thirty-five while Casey and the summer wait.

### What the three parts say together

Taken as one night, the dream is not three random stories. It is one long report on movement, belonging, desire, and direction.

In part one you help the group move, then watch the bus leave without you. In part two you navigate walls to reach recreation, get distracted, catch up late, and feel compared to another man at the end of the day. In part three you plan a good day with a friend in a place tied to your past, take a wrong turn toward a highway, see the dead end on the map, then meet the chain-link fence and a Hudson Valley overlook through the windshield before a flat tire stops you while your father speaks the wrong number about your car.

The through-line is not that you cannot move. You move constantly. The through-line is that you rarely see yourself fully inside the main action you are traveling toward. You are in the bus but do not remember driving. You ski but do not see yourself skiing. You drive in New York but need the map first and the windshield second before the wrong ramp becomes fully real. Progress keeps happening around you, and you keep arriving a little late, a little outside, or a little misread.

The emotional through-line matters too. Alone after the bus leaves. Enjoyment when desire comes toward you without a chase. Annoyance when another man seems to get the pairing or attention you wanted. Frustration when the road system and then the tire stop a day that was supposed to be fun.

### What this may be about in waking life

If you want a plain waking read, this is one coherent picture.

You are in the maze phase again, but now with snow and a bus instead of rain and tunnels. You are volunteering more than you used to, especially in your own business, and part of you fears that when the vehicle finally moves it will move without you or without giving you the seat you earned by helping.

You want reciprocity, not only usefulness. The woman who asks for the kiss may be the psyche balancing the bus scene with an image of being wanted directly. That connects to partnership desires, client yeses, audience for the membership site, and the Belize or South Pacific trips where the question is not only "can I afford it" but "can I let good things come toward me without over-preparing or bowing out."

The ski trio may be about group outings, friendship triangles, or any situation where you do the work of getting over the wall and still feel second at the end of the day. The scrape on the passenger side where you stood is a mark of the day. The car still runs. Caution is wise, but the damage did not total the vehicle.

The Casey section is developed in the part three analysis above. In one line for the whole night: you want pleasure and companionship without rivalry, you refuse the highway scale, the dead end shows you home country you cannot drive through, and maintenance plus your father's wrong metric meet you at the shop when you finally stop to orient.

None of that proves one single symbol is the answer. The dream is showing you the shape of the month: help, exclusion, desire, comparison, wrong turns, good map reading, and a flat tire at the moment you decide to stop and orient.

### What may be asking for attention now

You do not need to decode every image to use the dream. Three lines stand out as practical.

First, notice where you are driving shifts for systems that may not keep you on board the next day. Your company is the obvious place. Are you building something that carries you, or only volunteering for a bus that leaves?

Second, notice where the map already shows a dead end but you still speed up the ramp until the fence appears in the windshield. Belize hoarding, business-class vetoes, membership perfection loops, and waiting for the perfect client yes can all work that way. The overview knows before the body stops.

Third, fix the flat tire before arguing about the number 18. The dream puts you at the shop. Help is there. The wrong mileage story from your father is a distraction from the actual repair. Something in waking life may need maintenance, not a better debate about whether you measure up.
