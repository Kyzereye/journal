# Journal — 12 July 2026

## Stuck in particular places

I have been thinking about why I find myself stuck in particular parts of my life. I decided to focus on one and see where that took me. I have been kind of pudgy most of my life. I never really saw it because I can still run and jump, and still do most of the things that would be considered play. But there have been weight levels that I seem to be stuck at. Right now, 180 lbs seems to be the main one. Get a little below that and I start craving junk food. Get a little above that and I am not really that hungry.

## Casey

I had a thought about my friend Casey. She is a very big girl. For most of the time we have known each other she has had a basically mistrust of men and relationships in general. I have concluded that her size is due to this mistrust of men. Even though she would like to be in a relationship, she still thinks men are bad, for the lack of a better word. So how to keep men from hitting on her? Put on weight. Also, by putting on weight, a certain subsection of men may be the only ones flirting with her—the subsection that may not be trustworthy, in my humble opinion.

## Am I doing the same?

So I asked myself: Am I doing the same? Growing up, I was like most 18 or 19 year old men who were interested in girls and sex. I was awkward and shy, very shy, but I came to believe that girls didn't like sex and emotions were more important. That girls put up with guys and sex because they, the girls, were looking for the emotional part. That went along well with my shyness, not being able to talk to women, and keeping a low profile so I wouldn't be ridiculed or made fun of. There is a days file that talks more about this.

So now I am realizing that I too may have kept on some weight because although I want to be attractive enough so I can meet a nice woman for a girlfriend, the insecurities mentioned have helped me keep some weight on so that I am not—or at the very least, I do not think I am and put out that negative energy.

## Gym, food, yoga

I will do cardio and lift weights in an effort to trim down and look better, but it is like the food. If I start going to the gym and working out, I will start to see some results, but then something happens like I have too much to do, I hurt myself, or I am just bored of it and I stop and settle back into the same body state.

There is also yoga. I have enjoyed yoga in the past and I have seen positive results from continually doing yoga, but after a few days of doing it, I lose interest and I go back to the same state.

## Analysis

**Assistant:** The weight is probably not the problem. It is the solution your psyche settled on when wanting a woman and fearing exposure could not both be true at once.

Freud called this secondary gain. Horney called it moving toward and away at the same time. Either way, 180 pounds keeps you in a workable compromise. You stay eligible for relationship without having to find out whether you are actually wanted.

The cravings below 180 are worth treating as data, not weakness. In IFS terms, a protector is likely stepping in when you get too close to visibility. The job of that part is not to ruin your diet. It is to prevent the test you have been avoiding for decades.

The old belief that women tolerate male desire rather than share it is doing more work than the scale is. It turned sexual wanting into something shameful, and the body followed. Weight became a way to look like you are playing the game while still hiding the part of you that wants to win.

The gym and yoga pattern is probably resistance, not poor discipline. Change becomes real, anxiety rises, and the mind labels it boredom or inconvenience. That is a common way the older strategy reasserts itself without you having to admit you pulled back on purpose.

The useful question is not how to get below 180. It is what you expect would happen if you did, looked better, and had to be visibly desiring without the buffer. My guess is the fear is not rejection alone. It is finding out whether the adolescent story was ever true.

Start there. When cravings or avoidance spike, ask what showing up as a sexual, visible man would cost you. That is likely where the work actually is.

## Analysis — confidence, exterior validation, and the pull

**Assistant:** You are not contradicting yourself. You are naming two layers that usually run at the same time.

Rogers would call the first layer conditions of worth: the idea that you are only allowed to feel good about yourself after you earn it through appearance or achievement. The second layer is more honest and more human. You want to feel better in your own skin, and you also want that feeling to be visible to other people. Horney would say the tension is between a real wish and an idealized image of how a person is supposed to value themselves.

The loop you described is not hypocrisy. It is how self-esteem often gets built in practice. Body changes, mood shifts, energy changes, other people respond differently, and that response reinforces the mood. William James would treat that as normal. The problem is not that exterior feedback matters. The problem is when exterior feedback becomes the only proof that you are allowed to feel good.

So "getting in shape for me" is partly true and partly incomplete. Health and confidence are real motives. Attraction and being seen are also real motives. Admitting the second part does not make the first part fake. It makes the goal clearer.

This is the more important piece, and the psychological tradition has a lot to say about it.

Freud would not be surprised at all. He spent his career on cases where people understood the pattern and repeated it anyway. Insight lives in one part of the mind. The pull toward ice cream or fast food lives in another. Knowing better does not mean the wanting part has been heard or satisfied.

Jung would call that a complex with its own momentum. You can watch it operate and still not be the one steering in that moment.

Internal Family Systems would frame it even more directly. One part goes to the store with a plan. Another part feels the pull and chooses relief, comfort, or rebellion. The second part is not stupid. It is doing a job, usually protecting you from tension, deprivation, visibility, or the feeling of being "on program."

Beck would add that the grocery store is a bad place to fight this with logic alone. By the time you feel the pull, the emotional system is already ahead of the rational one. The justifications you mentioned, buying now because it is lunchtime or because you might be hungry later, are classic permission thoughts. They let the behavior happen while keeping you from feeling fully responsible for the choice.

Marlatt's relapse research is useful here too. The dangerous moment is often not the craving itself. It is the small decision that looks harmless, like "just this once" or "I'll get back on track tomorrow." Once that door opens, the old pattern has room to run.

The fact that you can see it happening matters, but Yalom would say awareness by itself changes less than people hope. What you are describing is not blindness. It is a moment where one version of you knows the direction and another version still chooses the familiar exit.

Do not treat those moments as proof that you lack discipline. Treat them as contact with the part of you that still thinks safety, comfort, or avoidance is more urgent than the plan.

When the pull shows up at the store, the useful question is not "Why am I weak?" It is "What is this food buying me right now?" Relief from pressure. A break from trying. A way to stay at the familiar weight. A small act of rebellion against a standard you do not fully believe you deserve to meet.

That is where the work is. Not in another lecture about healthy eating. In catching the moment and naming what the choice is actually for.
